📋 Case Background:

A 66-year-old male patient with end-stage renal disease, undergoing hemodialysis every Tuesday, Thursday, and Saturday at KMUH, was transferred from the emergency room to the neurological intensive care unit on March 22 due to confusion, consciousness disturbance, and intermittent jerky movements.

Present Illness:

His ADL was partially dependent, and he walked with a walker after a traffic accident 2 years ago. Spinal lesion was denied. He lives alone; a friend visits almost every day after work around 9 PM and helps with bathing and housekeeping.

According to his friend, he has been retired from taxi driving and using a walker since the traffic accident 2 years ago. He visited a local clinic on 3/18 and was told he had T-spine herpes zoster. Oral medication, 2 tablets five times daily, was prescribed.

He first felt general discomfort on 3/19 during hemodialysis at KMUH and complained of a skin rash on his back. Hemodialysis was stopped 1.5 hours early due to discomfort. On 3/20, he complained of dizziness, general soreness, and right chest pain. There was no headache, fever, neck pain, new focal weakness, or recent URI, UTI, or GI upset symptoms.

His friend mentioned personality change and hallucinations after he moved into a new house 3 months ago. No seizure-like episode had been noticed. TOCC, alcohol use, and illicit drug use were denied.

He visited the ED on the morning of 3/20 due to right chest pain. Initial status was E4V5M6, T 36.1°C, pulse 100/min, and respiratory rate 18/min. Morphine was used, and Gabapentin 100 mg QID, acyclovir 200 mg QID, and Ultracet 1 tablet QID were prescribed for T2-3 herpes zoster; then he was discharged.

After returning home, irrelevant speech and mild agitation were found, with vomiting 3 times. There was no headache, neck pain, double vision, decreased consciousness, head injury, or limb convulsion. He was brought back to the ED in the afternoon on the same day.

At the ED, initial vital signs showed T 37.0°C, pulse 91/min, respiratory rate 20/min, BP 196/87 mmHg, E4V4M5-6, and finger-stick glucose 340 mg/dL. EKG showed normal sinus rhythm. Lab data showed no leukocytosis or elevated CRP, but NT-proBNP was >70000.0 and mild hypercapnia was noted (CO2 49.7, pH 7.375).

Brain CT showed no hemorrhage, dilated ventricles suspected to be related to brain atrophy, and a right medial frontal small hypodense lesion. Chest X-ray revealed bilateral pleural effusion (R>L) and bilateral increased infiltration.

Agitation and irrelevant speech persisted, associated with irregular limb convulsions and stereotypic movements including bilateral upper limb circling, lower limb kicking, chewing, or spitting. Delirium was suspected. He ate a small amount for dinner without choking. Haloperidol was prescribed due to agitation, and he slept well. However, consciousness disturbance with intermittent jerky limb movements was found the next morning. Under the impression of CNS infection, Neurology was consulted and he was admitted to the NICU for further management.
